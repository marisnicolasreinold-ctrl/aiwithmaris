Understanding hallucinations: why AI confidently makes things up

31 May 2026 · 2 min read · Maris Reinold

Imagine asking an AI about a court ruling on employment law — and getting a convincing answer with case number, date and quote. It all sounds credible. Except: the ruling doesn’t exist. The AI invented it. Without hesitation, without a warning label.

This behaviour is called hallucination, and anyone deploying AI in a company should understand it — not to get scared, but to build the right guardrails.

Why does this happen at all?

A language model is not a reference book. It has no database of facts to look things up in. It has learned which words plausibly follow each other in which context — and that is exactly what it produces: plausible text. Most of the time, plausible is also correct. But when the model doesn’t know something, it still generates the most likely answer — and that answer sounds just as confident as a correct one.

So hallucinating is not a flaw in the system. It is the system. Models are getting better at admitting uncertainty — but you shouldn’t rely on it.

Where it’s harmless — and where it isn’t

The risk question isn’t “does the AI hallucinate?” but “what happens when it does?”

  • Harmless: draft texts, brainstorming, summaries that an expert reviews. An invented detail gets noticed and cut.
  • Risky: numbers, legal matters, medical content, commitments to customers — anything that leaves the building unchecked or triggers decisions.

Four guardrails for practice

  • Require sources: connect the AI to your real documents and have it answer from them — with a pointer to the passage. Then every statement can be verified.
  • Human before send: everything that reaches customers is seen by a person first. Generating becomes checking — still far faster than writing from scratch.
  • Ban the delicate questions: define the areas where the AI shouldn’t answer at all (legal advice, price commitments) but refer to a human instead.
  • Ask instead of believe: the simplest check takes one second: “How do you know that?” Shaky sources get looked up.

AI is a brilliant, fast colleague with one weakness: it never likes admitting it doesn’t know something. Know that, build your processes around it, and you get the benefit — without the nasty surprises.
