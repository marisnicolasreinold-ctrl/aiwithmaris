Why your team doesn’t use AI tools — and what coaching changes

27 May 2026 · 2 min read · Maris Reinold

A picture I see often: a company buys AI licences for the whole team. There’s a kickoff session, everyone nods. Three months later the statistics show: a handful of people use the tool daily — the rest never logged in again.

The reflex then is: “Our people just aren’t tech-savvy.” That’s almost never true. The real reasons are more mundane — and fixable.

Reason 1: nobody has time to experiment

AI tools don’t reveal their value by being watched, but by being tried on your own work. And that’s exactly what daily business leaves no time for. Whoever has five minutes between two meetings falls back into the old routine — it works, after all. Without protected time to practise, every tool remains a foreign object.

Reason 2: the examples don’t fit

Standard trainings show standard examples: poems, travel planning, marketing copy. The clerk who handles supplier complaints all day sees — rightly — no connection to her work. The value has to become visible at one’s own desk, otherwise it stays theory.

Reason 3: the quiet fear

The unspoken thought of many employees: “If the AI can do my work, what am I still needed for?” Anyone thinking that has a rational interest in the tool failing. This worry doesn’t disappear by being ignored — it disappears through honest conversations about what changes and what doesn’t.

What coaching does differently than training

A training is an appointment. Coaching is a process — and for me it looks like this:

  • On real tasks: everyone brings one of their own annoying work items. That’s what we practise on — not examples from the internet.
  • In small doses: four short sessions over weeks beat one eight-hour day that nothing sticks from.
  • With follow-ups: the real questions surface after two weeks of practice. That’s exactly when someone needs to be there to answer them.
  • With multipliers: every team has one or two curious people. Strengthen them and you get internal go-to people who remain when the coach is gone.

The tool is the smallest part of the change. Software brings the capability into the building — coaching brings it into people’s heads. Only both together change the everyday.
