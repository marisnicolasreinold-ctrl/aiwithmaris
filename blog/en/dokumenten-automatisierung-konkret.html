Document automation in practice: what happens when nobody types up invoices anymore?

12 June 2026 · 2 min read · Maris Reinold

Picture the desk in accounting. On the left, the pile: incoming invoices — PDFs in the inbox, scans, sometimes still paper. On the right, the ERP system. In between, a person transferring data: supplier, invoice number, date, amounts, cost centre. All day. Every day.

That middle step is one of the most rewarding cases for AI in a company. But what does “automating” actually mean here? Let’s look inside the engine room.

Step 1: Capture

Everything that comes in lands in one place — an email inbox, a scan folder. Even this unspectacular step creates order: no more “is that on your desk or mine?”.

Step 2: Extract

The AI reads the document and pulls out the fields — supplier, numbers, line items, amounts. The difference from classic OCR: modern models don’t need a rigid template per supplier. They handle layouts they’ve never seen before.

Step 3: Validate

Do the totals add up? Does the supplier exist? Does the invoice match an order? This is where the quality of the whole thing is decided — not in the extraction.

Step 4: Propose, don’t wave through

The software presents a ready-made booking proposal. A person looks at it and confirms. “Typing up” becomes “checking” — and that’s the real win: seconds instead of minutes per document, with attention going to the cases that deserve it.

What changes for the team

The honest answer: the work doesn’t disappear, it shifts. Instead of transferring data, the team resolves edge cases, follows up with suppliers, keeps the master data clean. That’s more demanding work than typing — and in my experience, nobody misses the pile.

Where the limits are

So you don’t start with the wrong expectations: handwritten notes on documents remain difficult. Very exotic documents need more corrections at first. And a rate of 100% automatically processed documents is not the goal — the goal is that the broad normal case flows through and people handle the rest. Start with that expectation and you’ll rarely be disappointed.

If you’re looking for a first AI use case: the paper pile is almost always a good candidate. It’s measurable, it annoys everyone — and the benefit shows up within weeks, not years.
