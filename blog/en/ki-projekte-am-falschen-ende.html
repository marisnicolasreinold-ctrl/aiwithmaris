Why most AI projects in mid-sized companies start at the wrong end

10 June 2026 · 2 min read · Maris Reinold

I’ve seen it often enough to recognise the pattern: a company decides it’s “time to do something with AI”. A strategy workshop follows. Then a tool selection. Then a steering committee. Six months later there’s a slide deck — and not a single process that runs better than before.

Most AI projects don’t fail because of the technology. They fail because they start at the wrong end.

The reflex to tackle the big problem

It sounds logical: if we’re introducing AI anyway, let’s do it where it matters most. So you pick the biggest problem. That’s exactly the mistake. The biggest problem has the most dependencies, the most stakeholders, the highest risk — and the longest road before anyone sees a benefit. Perfect conditions for driving a project into the wall before it has learned to walk.

The most annoying small problem beats the big strategic one

Better: find the problem everyone groans about. The invoices someone types up by hand. The enquiries that sit unanswered for three days. The list that gets copied together from three systems every Monday.

Processes like these are small enough to automate in weeks — and visible enough that everyone in the building notices the difference afterwards. And then something interesting happens: the next department comes to you on its own, asking whether this could work for them too. One project turns into a pull. No strategy paper achieves that.

How to recognise the right first process

Ask five questions about every candidate:

  • Does it happen daily or weekly — not just three times a year?
  • Does it noticeably cost time or nerves, for more than one person?
  • Can you clearly define what “done correctly” means?
  • Does the process involve only a few systems and one department?
  • Would a mistake be annoying, but not existential?

Five yeses? You’ve found your starting point. Four is still good. Three or fewer: next candidate.

And the strategy?

It comes — but later, and then based on experience instead of guesswork. Once you’ve automated one small process, you know how your team actually works with AI, where your data quality pinches and what’s realistic. A strategy built on that is worth gold. One written beforehand is a guess.

Start where it counts. Prove the value. Then scale. In that order — not the other way around.
