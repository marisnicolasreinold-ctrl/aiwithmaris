Make or buy in AI: when to build custom software, when a ready-made tool will do

22 May 2026 · 2 min read · Maris Reinold

“Should we buy this or have it built?” — sooner or later this question comes up in almost every AI conversation. And the honest answer is uncomfortable for someone who builds software: buying is usually right. But not always. The difference sits in a place where few people look.

The one question that counts

Forget prices and feature lists for a moment. The decisive question is: is this process the same at your company as at a thousand others — or is it the reason customers buy from you?

Booking invoices, coordinating appointments, answering standard emails: everyone does these similarly. Ready-made tools exist for that, tested by thousands of users. Building your own here means spending serious money on something that already exists.

But the process that lets you quote faster than your competitors? The particular way you plan orders? That’s your edge. Squeeze it into a standard tool and you make yourself interchangeable — and gift your edge to the tool vendor, who sells it to everyone else tomorrow.

When buying is the right call

  • The process is standard — other industries do it the same way
  • Established tools exist with EU hosting and proper contracts
  • You need results quickly and want low maintenance
  • Customisation means settings, not rebuilds

When building pays off

  • The process is a genuine differentiator of your business
  • Ready-made tools would force you to bend your process
  • The data is sensitive enough that full control is mandatory
  • You have one clear, small use case — not an everything machine

The middle path many overlook

Make-or-buy sounds like either-or. In practice the best solution is often a third thing: ready-made building blocks, custom-wired. The AI model comes from a provider, the database is standard — but the logic in between, the part that mirrors your process, is tailor-made. That costs a fraction of a full custom build and still fits like a glove.

Rule of thumb to take away: buy the usual, build the special — and start small either way.
